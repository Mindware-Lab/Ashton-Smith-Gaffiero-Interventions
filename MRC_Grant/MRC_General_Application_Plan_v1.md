# MRC Proof of Concept Stage One: General Application Plan

## Working title

**AI-Augmented Transfer Engineering for Digital Cognitive-Affective Interventions: Proof of Concept in Chronic Primary Pain**

## 1. Proposed project

The project will develop and establish proof of concept for an **AI-assisted, mechanism-led digital cognitive-affective intervention for chronic primary pain**, while validating a computational research tool designed to reduce uncertainty and improve efficiency during intervention selection and optimisation.

The project has two integrated products: a pain-specific clinical intervention and a generalisable computational development technology, supported by a focused service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand. Chronic primary pain will provide the first full clinical instantiation and validation case for the reusable architecture.

### Clinical product

A prototype digital intervention targeting cognitive-affective mechanisms associated with pain-related interference and reduced functioning, including:

* threat-sensitive attention;
* interpretation of ambiguous bodily and situational information;
* uncertainty monitoring;
* inflexible evidence weighting;
* avoidance and activity decisions;
* difficulty updating predictions following safe or manageable outcomes.

### Development technology

The **HRP Transfer Lab**, a computational intervention-development engine combining:

* evidence and behavioural databases;
* auditable parameter priors;
* competing cognitive models;
* synthetic participant populations;
* synthetic experiments;
* model and parameter recovery;
* efficient human-study design;
* protected and delayed transfer tests.

The computational system will not be presented as replacing clinical research. Its purpose is to identify weak intervention designs early, optimise promising candidates, and make subsequent human studies smaller, denser and more informative. Although validated initially through chronic primary pain, the HRP Transfer Lab is designed as reusable research infrastructure. Its registries, modular model layers, synthetic-experiment methods, psychometric-development workflows, transfer-testing framework and governance systems could subsequently be adapted to other cognitive-affective health problems. Each new indication would require its own mechanism mapping, domain-specific evidence, task and measure validation, human calibration and clinical-pathway specification.


### Supporting service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand

A focused, human-reviewed pathway strand will determine where the intervention could add value within chronic-pain care, which patients it is intended for, which patients require more intensive or alternative support, and what practical constraints could obstruct adoption. It will use structured pathway mapping, stakeholder and lived-experience evidence, and AI-assisted synthesis of service documents where appropriate.

The strand will produce:

* a focused map of the relevant chronic-pain pathway;
* candidate patient entry and referral points;
* intended-use, exclusion and escalation criteria;
* practitioner-oversight and data-flow requirements;
* implementation constraints and adoption risks;
* a proposed future delivery model for NHS or other pain-service settings.

This is not intended to become a comprehensive NHS service-modelling programme or an automated allocation system. Its purpose is to place the prototype credibly within a real care pathway and strengthen the route to patient benefit.

## 2. Why the project fits the MRC Proof of Concept call

The MRC call supports academically led translational projects that:

* address significant health needs;
* develop digital healthcare or behavioural and psychological interventions;
* develop research tools that increase the efficiency of intervention development;
* progress from prototype development towards early clinical testing.

Applications must include sufficient proof-of-concept evidence and a credible development plan leading towards patient benefit.

The proposal therefore has a dual fit:

1. **Development of a novel digital cognitive-affective intervention** for a significant health problem.
2. **Development and validation of a computational research tool** intended to improve the efficiency of selecting and optimising intervention components.

A focused service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand strengthens this fit by specifying where the intervention should sit in the chronic-pain pathway, who should receive it, how clinical boundaries and escalation will operate, and what must be addressed before later adoption. This supporting strand will remain subordinate to the intervention-development objective rather than becoming a broad service-redesign programme.

Chronic primary pain provides a stronger lead indication than maths-exam anxiety because it offers:

* a clear clinical population;
* direct patient and functional outcomes;
* an identifiable NHS pathway;
* alignment with Dan Gaffiero’s publication record and expertise;
* established relevance of attention, interpretation, threat appraisal, prediction and avoidance;
* a credible route from proof of mechanism to patient benefit.

The application will remain focused on one clinical indication. However, chronic primary pain will serve as the first full demonstrator of a broader, domain-adaptive intervention-development architecture. The reusable value lies in the computational and translational workflow—not in assuming that pain-specific tasks, parameters or clinical findings transfer automatically to other populations.

Future applications could include adult ADHD, anxiety, addiction, depression, fatigue-related conditions and performance under pressure, but each would require a separate mechanism model, evidence base, measurement system, pathway specification and prospective validation programme.

## 3. Central development proposition

The proposed innovation is not simply another cognitive-training task.

The project will first define the intervention’s intended pathway position:

```text
current chronic-pain pathway
→ unmet or weakly supported point
→ intended intervention user
→ access or referral route
→ clinical exclusions and escalation
→ practitioner oversight and data flow
→ patient-relevant functional outcome
→ onward care
```

Within that bounded clinical use case, the project will test whether chronic-pain intervention development can be improved by engineering and validating a connected adaptive policy:

```text
attend to relevant information
→ distinguish signal from noise
→ infer uncertainty and reliability
→ revise threat or safety predictions
→ select an appropriate action
→ obtain environmental feedback
→ update the policy
→ recover it in a changed context and after delay
```

The intervention-development system will evaluate which combination of components most plausibly influences this chain.

Candidate components may include:

* neutral attention-control training;
* affectively salient attention-control challenges;
* adaptive evidence accumulation;
* interpretation and appraisal updating;
* activity prediction and decision-making;
* wrapper and context variation;
* real-world implementation missions;
* delayed transfer probes.

## 4. Primary aims

### Aim 1: Define the target product, pathway position and mechanism model

Develop a clinically credible target product profile and pathway-placement model for adults with chronic primary pain whose functioning is affected by maladaptive attention, appraisal, uncertainty monitoring and avoidance.

Outputs:

* target patient group;
* focused map of the relevant pain pathway and unmet-support point;
* intended use, entry point and delivery pathway;
* referral or self-access route;
* intervention components;
* contraindications, exclusions, escalation and safety boundaries;
* practitioner-oversight and data-flow requirements;
* implementation constraints and adoption risks;
* mechanism map;
* primary clinical and functional outcomes;
* preliminary commercial and implementation pathway.

### Aim 2: Build and validate the computational development engine

Develop and validate the reusable HRP Transfer Lab components required to:

* ingest relevant evidence and behavioural data;
* register parameter estimates with source traceability and transportability ratings;
* formalise competing cognitive-affective mechanisms;
* simulate heterogeneous synthetic participants;
* test parameter and model recoverability;
* optimise trial conditions and human-study designs;
* compare intervention candidates under explicit assumptions.

Outputs:

* prior registry;
* modular generative model;
* synthetic experiment engine;
* recovery and calibration report;
* reproducible evidence packets;
* recommended candidate protocols for human testing;
* documented domain-adaptation framework;
* reusable registry, modelling, simulation and psychometric-development schemas.

### Aim 3: Produce and refine a pain-specific digital prototype

Translate the selected mechanism chain into a working digital intervention.

A provisional sequence is:

```text
readiness and state check
→ controlled attention task
→ uncertainty-sensitive evidence task
→ pain-appraisal inference task
→ activity or avoidance decision
→ implementation intention
→ real-world mission
→ delayed re-check
```

Outputs:

* functioning digital prototype;
* versioned protocol;
* adaptive controller;
* intervention-fidelity telemetry;
* patient and practitioner interfaces at prototype level.

### Aim 4: Conduct a human mechanism-mapping and calibration study

Collect dense behavioural and acceptability data from people with chronic primary pain to estimate:

* task feasibility;
* baseline parameter distributions;
* relationships among attention, appraisal, uncertainty and pain interference;
* task and measure reliability;
* participant heterogeneity;
* model calibration;
* candidate intervention targets.

This study should calibrate the computational system rather than be represented as an efficacy trial.

### Aim 5: Conduct an early proof-of-concept comparison

Compare a small number of computationally prioritised intervention configurations against an appropriate active control.

Primary questions:

* Can the intended cognitive-affective mechanism be modified?
* Does change extend to a protected, untrained task?
* Is there preliminary evidence of improvement in pain interference, activity engagement or self-management?
* Is the intervention feasible, acceptable and safe?
* Do computational predictions correspond to observed human outcomes?

## 5. Focused service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand

This supporting strand will ensure that the intervention is designed for a defined point in the chronic-pain pathway rather than developed as a context-free digital product.

| Purpose | Core question | Contribution to the project |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Target placement** | Where in the chronic-pain pathway could a low-intensity digital intervention add value without displacing necessary care? | Strengthens the target product profile and clinical use case |
| **Patient-segment definition** | Which patients may appropriately receive the intervention, and who requires more intensive or alternative support? | Clarifies intended use, exclusions, escalation and safeguarding |
| **Adoption preparation** | What referral, workflow, staffing, information-governance and data-flow constraints could prevent implementation? | Strengthens the route to patient benefit and later commercialisation |

### Methods

The strand may use:

* focused pathway and process mapping;
* review of NICE guidance, local service specifications, referral criteria and patient information;
* stakeholder interviews and workshops;
* lived-experience evidence;
* review of recruitment, onboarding, practitioner-oversight, escalation and onward-care requirements;
* AI-assisted document synthesis and evidence extraction, with source traceability and human clinical review;
* comparison of a small number of plausible intervention-placement scenarios.

AI will support document synthesis and pattern identification only. It will not make automated eligibility, referral, commissioning or treatment-allocation decisions.

### Principal outputs

```text
current pathway
→ candidate support gap
→ intended user and access route
→ exclusions and escalation
→ practitioner oversight
→ data and workflow integration
→ expected patient and service value
→ onward evaluation and adoption route
```

Outputs will include:

* a focused pathway map;
* a candidate deployment-point analysis;
* intended-use, exclusion and escalation rules;
* a practitioner and patient journey specification;
* workflow and data-flow requirements;
* an implementation and adoption risk register;
* a provisional pathway-integration model for later NHS or partner testing.

### Scope boundary

The project will not attempt comprehensive NHS pain-system modelling, geospatial inequality mapping, automated resource allocation, ICB-wide optimisation or a multi-pathway service-redesign programme. Those capabilities belong to the wider Intervention-Intelligence Platform model and may be pursued in later applied research. The present purpose is to place one intervention credibly within one chronic-pain pathway.

## 7. Principal progression milestones

The call requires timed progression milestones with robust go/no-go criteria and milestone-specific costs.

### Milestone 1 — Development readiness

Proposed timing: end of the computational and clinical-specification phase.

**Go criteria:**

* target product profile and focused pathway-placement model approved by the clinical and PPI groups;
* intended entry point, exclusions, escalation and practitioner-oversight requirements specified;
* functioning prototype of the core task sequence;
* clinically acceptable safety and escalation protocol;
* model and parameter recovery meet prespecified parameter-specific criteria;
* at least two competing mechanisms are empirically distinguishable;
* statistical design supports an informative human calibration study;
* IP and data-access issues have a credible route to resolution.

**No-go or revise criteria:**

* target construct cannot be measured reliably;
* candidate mechanisms are not identifiable;
* prototype burden is unacceptable;
* patient contributors reject the intervention rationale or delivery format;
* no credible or clinically acceptable pathway position can be identified;
* clinical pathway or recruitment route is unavailable.

### Milestone 2 — Human calibration and candidate lock

Proposed timing: after WP4.

**Go criteria:**

* recruitment and retention are feasible;
* core task measures meet predefined reliability thresholds;
* model predictions adequately reproduce key human behaviour;
* at least one modifiable mechanism is associated with pain interference or functional outcomes;
* a candidate intervention and comparator can be selected prospectively;
* no material safety or acceptability problem is identified.

**No-go or revise criteria:**

* models fail out-of-sample predictive checks;
* tasks are dominated by floor, ceiling or device-timing problems;
* mechanisms show no relationship with relevant pain outcomes;
* the selected components cannot be distinguished from generic engagement effects.

### Milestone 3 — Project endpoint

**Progression criteria:**

* intended mechanism changes relative to active control;
* transfer occurs on a protected, untrained measure;
* effect does not depend only on trained-task practice;
* preliminary functional outcome is in the predicted direction;
* intervention is acceptable and sufficiently adhered to;
* delayed results justify continued development;
* the computational engine produces calibrated predictions or demonstrates precisely where its assumptions failed;
* clinical, pathway-integration, commercial and regulatory development routes are viable.

The project may conclude with:

* **Advance:** proceed to a larger clinical evaluation.
* **Revise:** modify components or target population and repeat proof-of-mechanism work.
* **Retire:** stop the clinical intervention while retaining the validated research tools or measures.

## 8. Working duration, costing and milestone-cost assumptions

### Provisional duration

The project is provisionally conceived as a **30–36-month programme**. This is a working development assumption rather than a fixed commitment. The final duration should be determined through detailed work-package costing, recruitment requirements, software development effort, NHS delivery costs, institutional review and the number of progression cycles required.

The MRC scheme places no formal ceiling on project duration or funding, but the requested timescale and resources must be fully justified. Projects are expected to begin one to six months after the funding decision, and MRC normally funds 80% of full economic cost, with permitted exceptions funded according to scheme rules.

### Costing principles

The budget should be constructed from the development plan rather than selected in advance. It should distinguish:

* computational and statistical development;
* software and research-engineering posts;
* clinical and research staff time;
* patient and public involvement;
* participant recruitment and reimbursement;
* NHS support, treatment or excess-treatment costs where applicable;
* data management, security and long-term preservation;
* external licences, data access or specialist consultancy;
* health economics and implementation work;
* accessibility, user-experience and prototype testing;
* dissemination and onward-development preparation.

The stage-one application should provide approximate outline costs. A detailed schedule of events cost attribution may be required at stage two where NHS costs arise.

### Milestone-specific cost structure

The project plan should ultimately attach an indicative cost to each major progression stage, because the MRC guidance asks applicants to connect deliverables, go/no-go criteria and approximate costs.

| Costed stage | Principal activity | Indicative costing purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Stage A — Development readiness** | Clinical specification, pathway placement, PPI, computational architecture, prototype and recovery testing | Establish the minimum viable development platform and determine whether human calibration is justified |
| **Stage B — Human calibration and candidate lock** | Dense mechanism-mapping study, model fitting, prior updating, prototype refinement and prospective candidate selection | Determine whether the proposed mechanisms are measurable, clinically relevant and suitable for intervention testing |
| **Stage C — Early proof of concept** | Active-controlled comparison, protected transfer, delayed assessment, functional signal, implementation and commercial planning | Determine whether the intervention should advance, be revised or be retired |

Detailed monetary estimates should be produced jointly with KCL pre-award, finance, the clinical service and the statistician after the study design and staff effort are specified.

## 14. How efficiency will be evaluated

The project should not promise unvalidated twofold or sixfold efficiency gains.

Instead, it should prospectively measure whether the computational methodology improves:

* time from evidence synthesis to locked protocol;
* number of candidate protocols retired before human testing;
* number of human trials required per retained candidate;
* trial information per participant-minute;
* parameter and model-recovery accuracy;
* accuracy of predicted human outcomes;
* frequency of floor, ceiling and non-identifiability problems;
* cost of reaching an advance/revise/retire decision;
* number of pathway-placement and implementation uncertainties resolved before later trials.

The resulting claim should be:

> The project will test whether computational screening and synthetic experimental design can make early cognitive-affective intervention development more efficient and informative than a predefined conventional reference workflow.

## 18. Contingency and onward funding strategy

The MRC Proof of Concept application should remain the primary route when the prototype, clinical partnership, computational evidence and preliminary human data are sufficiently mature. Other schemes should be treated as **parallel alternatives selected at the September readiness gate**, because most current deadlines occur before the MRC stage-one outcome would be known.

| Funding route | Best use in this programme | Current timing or scale | Strategic judgement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **MRC Proof of Concept: stage one** | Integrated computational development engine plus one chronic-primary-pain intervention and early human proof of concept | Closes **18 November 2026**; no formal duration or funding ceiling; normally 80% FEC | **Primary target** when the full readiness package is credible |
| **MRC Impact Acceleration Award** | One critical, high-risk development step, such as model validation, prototype de-risking, psychometric calibration or preliminary pain data | **£50,000–£300,000**; closes **11 November 2026** | Best smaller route when one major technical or translational uncertainty prevents a full PoC bid |
| **NIHR Programme Development Grant** | PPI, pathway work, co-design, feasibility preparation and development of a later programme grant | Up to **£250,000** over **12–24 months**; current round closes **16 September 2026** | Best route when the wider programme still requires preparatory clinical and implementation development |
| **NIHR Research for Patient Benefit** | Focused NHS-facing feasibility, pilot or relatively near-term patient-benefit study in chronic pain | Current round closes **28 October 2026**; funding tiers include up to **£300,000** for preparatory trials and up to **£500,000** for nearer-term patient-benefit research | Strong alternative once a focused NHS study and eligible NHS-linked leadership structure are ready |
| **NIHR Health and Social Care Delivery Research** | Pain-pathway bottlenecks, service integration, workflow, adoption or organisational evaluation | Current researcher-led round closes **16 September 2026** | Appropriate only if service delivery becomes the primary research question rather than the intervention product |
| **MRC Experimental Medicine: stage one** | A focused human experimental study testing a precise cognitive-affective pain mechanism with an intervention or challenge | Closes **7 October 2026** | Strong mechanistic alternative if the product-development platform remains premature |
| **Later NIHR EME, HTA, i4i or PGfAR routes** | Efficacy, health technology assessment, product development, implementation and multisite programmes after successful proof of concept | Future follow-on routes | Should form the onward-development pathway, not the immediate fallback |

### Funding-portfolio principle

```text
MRC Proof of Concept
= integrated translational product and development engine

MRC Impact Acceleration
= one de-risking step

NIHR PDG
= preparatory programme development

NIHR RfPB
= focused NHS patient-benefit or feasibility study

NIHR HSDR
= pathway and service-delivery research

MRC Experimental Medicine
= focused human mechanistic experiment
```

## 19. September funding-route decision gate

The September readiness review should produce a documented choice of funding route rather than merely a general go/no-go decision.

| Readiness position by early September | Recommended route |
| --- | --- |
| Working pain prototype, successful parameter/model recovery, clinical pain partner, initial PPI, credible recruitment route and preliminary human calibration evidence | Proceed to **MRC Proof of Concept** |

* evidence available for each minimum requirement;

* estimated probability of completing a competitive application by each deadline;

* risk of diluting the main proposal through parallel applications;
* reasons for proceeding, deferring or selecting another route.

The funding-route decision should be agreed with KCL pre-award, the clinical partner, the quantitative lead and the project leads.
