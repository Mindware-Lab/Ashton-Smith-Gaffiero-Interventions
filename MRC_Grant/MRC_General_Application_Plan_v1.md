
# MRC Proof of Concept Stage One: General Application Plan

## Working title

**AI-Augmented Transfer Engineering for Digital Cognitive-Affective Interventions: Proof of Concept in Chronic Primary Pain**

# 1. Proposed project

The project will establish proof of concept for a computationally guided, mechanism-led digital cognitive-affective intervention for chronic primary pain. The intervention will be developed using the HRP Transfer Lab, an enabling computational research environment designed to reduce uncertainty and improve efficiency during intervention selection, optimisation and early evaluation.

The project has one primary clinical product: a pain-specific digital cognitive-affective intervention for chronic primary pain.

The HRP Transfer Lab will serve as the enabling computational development technology used to select, optimise and evaluate the intervention components. A focused service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand will support clinical placement, implementation planning and the route to patient benefit.

Chronic primary pain will provide the first full clinical instantiation and prospective evaluation of the reusable development architecture.

## 1.1 Clinical product

The clinical product will be a prototype digital intervention targeting cognitive-affective mechanisms associated with pain-related interference and reduced functioning. Candidate mechanisms include:

* threat-sensitive attention;
* interpretation of ambiguous bodily and situational information;
* uncertainty monitoring;
* inflexible evidence weighting;
* avoidance and activity decisions;
* difficulty updating predictions following safe, manageable or less adverse outcomes.

The intervention will be designed to support adaptive attention, interpretation, decision-making, self-management and engagement in meaningful activity. It will not be presented as replacing multidisciplinary pain care or as implying that persistent pain is unreal or reducible to faulty thinking.

## 1.2 Development technology

The HRP Transfer Lab will be developed as a computational intervention-development engine combining:

* scientific, behavioural and intervention databases;
* auditable parameter priors;
* competing cognitive and cognitive-affective models;
* empirically constrained simulated participant populations;
* simulation-based experiments;
* model and parameter recovery;
* human-study design optimisation;
* protected, cross-context and delayed transfer tests.

The computational system will not replace clinical research or prospective human experimentation. Its purpose is to identify weak intervention designs early, optimise promising candidates and test whether computational screening can make subsequent human studies smaller, denser and more informative.

Although initially instantiated and evaluated through chronic primary pain, the HRP Transfer Lab is intended to provide reusable research infrastructure. Its registries, modular model layers, simulation methods, psychometric-development workflows, transfer-testing framework and governance systems could subsequently be adapted to other cognitive-affective health problems.

Each new indication would require its own:

* mechanism mapping;
* domain-specific evidence base;
* task and measure development;
* human calibration;
* clinical-pathway specification;
* safety and governance assessment;
* prospective validation programme.

## 1.3 Supporting service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand

A focused, human-reviewed pathway strand will determine:

* where the intervention could add value within chronic-pain care;
* which patients it is intended for;
* which patients require more intensive or alternative support;
* how users could access the intervention;
* what practitioner oversight would be required;
* what practical constraints could obstruct implementation.

The strand will use structured pathway mapping, stakeholder and lived-experience evidence, and AI-assisted synthesis of service documents where appropriate.

It will produce:

* a focused map of the relevant chronic-pain pathway;
* candidate intervention entry and referral points;
* intended-use criteria;
* exclusion and escalation criteria;
* practitioner-oversight requirements;
* data-flow and information-governance requirements;
* implementation constraints and adoption risks;
* a proposed future delivery model for NHS or other pain-service settings.

The strand will not attempt comprehensive NHS service modelling, automated patient allocation or system-wide commissioning optimisation. Its purpose is to position the prototype credibly within a real care pathway and strengthen the route to patient benefit.

## 1.4 Preliminary enabling study and platform de-risking

A proposed parallel BPS Small Research Grant feasibility study, led by Dr Daniel Gaffiero with Dr Mark Ashton Smith as co-applicant, is intended to provide an initial empirical implementation of the transfer-protocol engineering methodology.

The study adapts the masked Majority Function Task from absolute feature judgements to relational judgements. It examines whether training transfers directionally between absolute and relative cognitive-control conditions in university students reporting moderate-to-high mathematics anxiety.

The design includes:

* an active-control condition;
* repeated digital training;
* pre-, mid-, post- and follow-up assessments;
* comparison of absolute and relative cognitive-control training;
* an independent arithmetic-performance outcome under evaluative pressure.

Although the study addresses mathematics anxiety rather than chronic primary pain, it tests platform-level capabilities directly relevant to the proposed MRC programme:

* remote delivery of an adaptive cognitive intervention;
* trial-level behavioural and timing instrumentation;
* separation of trained-task improvement from relational transfer;
* estimation of learning curves, transfer asymmetry and delayed recovery;
* preliminary psychometric, usability and acceptability assessment;
* generation of empirically constrained parameter distributions for computational modelling and simulation-based experimental optimisation.

The study will therefore be treated as an **enabling calibration study**, not as evidence for the efficacy or clinical validity of the chronic-pain intervention.

Its design—and, subject to ethics, consent and data-governance arrangements, any resulting data—may inform:

```text
learning-curve estimation
→ transfer-cost modelling
→ parameter and model recovery
→ calibration of simulated populations
→ protocol-schedule optimisation
→ design of the chronic-primary-pain human studies
```

All pain-specific mechanisms, task adaptations, clinical interpretations and functional outcomes will nevertheless be developed and prospectively validated in adults with chronic primary pain.

## 1.5 Developmental architecture

The enabling mathematics-anxiety study and the proposed MRC programme represent two sequential but scientifically distinct stages of the wider transfer-engineering programme.

```text
ENABLING FEASIBILITY STUDY

Mathematics-anxiety study
→ test remote digital delivery
→ calibrate transfer-sensitive measures
→ estimate human learning and variability
→ evaluate the computational workflow

                         ↓

MRC CLINICAL DEVELOPMENT PROGRAMME

Pain-specific intervention
+ HRP Transfer Lab
+ chronic-primary-pain mechanism mapping
+ human model calibration
+ active-controlled proof of concept
+ functional and delayed outcomes
```

The developmental sequence is:

```text
generic transfer-engineering capability
first implemented in an accessible, lower-risk population
→ computational and measurement methodology refined
→ condition-specific clinical architecture developed
→ chronic-primary-pain proof of concept conducted
```

The enabling study contributes methodological and platform-level learning. It does not provide direct evidence for pain-specific mechanisms, clinical efficacy or functional benefit. Those questions will be addressed prospectively within the chronic-primary-pain programme.

The MRC-funded product is the chronic-primary-pain intervention. The HRP Transfer Lab is the enabling development technology, the pathway strand supports translation and implementation, and the BPS study provides separate methodological calibration. Success of the MRC programme will ultimately be judged by whether it produces a clinically credible pain intervention and a justified advance, revise or retire decision.

# 2. Fit with the MRC Proof of Concept call

The project combines a defined clinical product, a reusable computational development technology and an enabling human feasibility study that begins to de-risk the proposed transfer-engineering methodology.

The enabling study contributes platform-level feasibility and calibration evidence. It does not substitute for pain-specific proof-of-concept evidence.

The MRC Proof of Concept scheme supports academically led translational projects that:

* address significant health needs;
* develop digital healthcare interventions;
* develop behavioural or psychological interventions;
* develop research tools that improve the efficiency of intervention development;
* progress from prototype development towards early clinical testing.

Applications must present sufficient supporting evidence for their current development stage and a credible pathway towards patient benefit.

The proposal has two primary areas of fit:

1. **Development of a novel digital cognitive-affective intervention** for a significant health and functional problem.
2. **Development and prospective evaluation of a computational research tool** intended to improve the efficiency of selecting, optimising and testing intervention components.

The focused service-pathway and implementation-intelligence strand further strengthens the translational case by specifying:

* where the intervention would sit in the chronic-pain pathway;
* who the intended users would be;
* who should not receive the intervention without additional support;
* how escalation and onward referral would operate;
* what practitioner oversight would be required;
* which implementation barriers must be resolved before wider adoption.

This strand will remain subordinate to the intervention-development objective and will not become a broad service-redesign programme.

Chronic primary pain provides a stronger lead indication than mathematics anxiety for this MRC application because it offers:

* a clearly defined clinical population;
* direct patient-relevant and functional outcomes;
* an identifiable NHS care pathway;
* alignment with Dr Daniel Gaffiero’s research on pain-related interpretation and cognitive-affective processes;
* established relevance of attention, interpretation, threat appraisal, prediction and avoidance;

The application will remain focused on one clinical indication. Chronic primary pain will serve as the first full demonstrator of a broader, domain-adaptive intervention-development architecture.

The reusable value lies in the computational and translational workflow. The application will not assume that pain-specific tasks, parameters or clinical findings generalise automatically to other populations.

Potential later applications include:

* adult ADHD;

# 3. Scientific background and preliminary evidence

## 3.1 Chronic primary pain as a significant health and functional problem

Chronic primary pain is associated with substantial and persistent effects on daily functioning, activity, work, relationships, emotional wellbeing and quality of life. For many people, the central burden is not captured adequately by pain intensity alone. Pain interference, avoidance, loss of valued activity, uncertainty and reduced confidence in self-management may remain severe even where medical investigation or treatment has been completed.

Existing pain-management and rehabilitation services commonly combine psychological, physiotherapeutic, occupational and self-management approaches. These services can be valuable, but access, format, intensity and availability vary. Some patients experience long waits, fragmented care or limited access to psychologically informed support.

There is therefore a need for scalable, low-intensity digital adjuncts that:

* complement rather than replace existing care;
* support adaptive functioning and self-management;
* can be accessed at an appropriate pathway point;
* have clear exclusion and escalation criteria;
* target explicitly defined mechanisms;
* generate evidence about which components are useful for whom.

The proposed intervention will focus primarily on functioning, activity engagement, adaptive interpretation and self-management rather than treating reduction in reported pain intensity as the sole measure of success.

## 3.2 Cognitive-affective mechanisms relevant to functioning

The proposed intervention is based on a connected cognitive-affective mechanism chain:

```text
attention to bodily and contextual information
→ interpretation of ambiguity
→ estimation of uncertainty and reliability
→ threat or safety prediction
→ avoidance or activity decision
→ environmental feedback
→ belief and policy updating
```

Threat-sensitive attention may increase the salience of pain-related information and reduce access to competing contextual or safety information. Ambiguous bodily sensations and situations may then be interpreted in ways that increase perceived threat, uncertainty or anticipated harm.

These interpretations can influence predictions about activity, symptom escalation and personal capability. Where threat predictions remain rigid or are updated insufficiently following safe or manageable experiences, avoidance may be maintained even when an activity could be attempted safely within appropriate clinical guidance.

The programme will investigate several linked processes:

* pain-related interpretation bias;
* threat-sensitive attention;
* memory for pain-related and safety-related information;
* uncertainty monitoring;
* reliability-sensitive evidence accumulation;
* expectations about activity and future symptoms;
* avoidance and activity selection;
* updating following safe, tolerable or manageable outcomes;
* self-management confidence.

The project will not assume that all participants share a single cognitive-affective profile. The computational and human calibration work will examine whether distinct combinations of mechanisms characterise different participants and whether these differences imply different intervention priorities.

## 3.3 Limitations of current intervention-development approaches

Behavioural and psychological interventions frequently combine multiple components, such as attention exercises, interpretation tasks, psychoeducation, goal setting, activity planning and self-management strategies. When such packages are evaluated only as complete programmes, it can be difficult to determine:

* which component influenced the intended mechanism;
* which component produced only local task learning;
* which component contributed to functional change;
* which participants benefited from which component;
* which elements added burden without adding value;
* which components should be retained, revised or retired.

Early intervention studies may also rely heavily on average treatment effects and outcomes closely related to the trained activity. This can obscure whether the intervention produces:

* genuine mechanism change;
* transfer to an untrained measure;
* generalisation across contexts;
* improvement in meaningful functioning;
* persistence after delay.

Large human studies may consequently be launched before task reliability, parameter identifiability, intervention dosage and outcome sensitivity have been resolved.

The proposed project addresses this development gap by integrating computational modelling, simulation-based study optimisation, dense human calibration and staged transfer testing before progression to larger clinical evaluation.

## 3.4 Scientific rationale for transfer-protocol engineering

The project distinguishes five levels of evidence:

```text
trained-task improvement
≠ mechanism change
≠ protected transfer
≠ functional transfer
≠ delayed retention
```

A participant may become more proficient at the exact training task without acquiring a portable cognitive-affective policy. Conversely, an intervention may produce a modest local training effect while improving performance on an independent or more functionally relevant task.

The project will therefore evaluate candidate components through a staged transfer sequence:

```text
trained operation
→ new task parameters
→ changed wrapper or context
→ independent task
→ functional outcome
→ delayed re-check
```

Protected transfer measures will be selected or constructed so that they are not repeatedly trained during the intervention. Where appropriate, active controls and mechanistic comparators will help distinguish specific mechanism change from:

* expectancy;
* repeated assessment;
* engagement;
* general digital contact;
* non-specific practice effects.

Model comparison will be used to test whether observed behavioural changes are more consistent with the intended mechanism than with plausible alternatives.

Delayed reassessment will test whether any transferred operation remains recoverable after recent practice and prompting have diminished.

## 3.5 Computational and AI-assisted development rationale

The HRP Transfer Lab will use computational modelling and AI-assisted research methods to reduce avoidable uncertainty before and during human intervention development.

The computational architecture will include:

* auditable parameter priors derived from published and available behavioural evidence;
* competing generative models of attention, interpretation, uncertainty, evidence weighting and action selection;
* empirically constrained simulated participant populations;
* parameter-recovery and model-recovery studies;
* simulated comparisons of intervention schedules;
* detection of floor, ceiling and non-identifiability problems;
* optimisation of task conditions and trial allocation;
* prospective predictions for human calibration and proof-of-concept studies.

AI-assisted evidence tools may support literature surveillance, evidence extraction, parameter-source tracing and comparison of candidate intervention components. All clinically consequential interpretations, protocol specifications and progression decisions will remain subject to human scientific and clinical review.

Simulation will support intervention selection and experimental design. It will not establish clinical efficacy, determine eligibility for treatment or replace prospective validation with patients.

The project will evaluate whether the computational workflow improves early intervention development relative to a predefined conventional reference workflow. Relevant outcomes will include:

* time from evidence synthesis to a locked protocol;
* number of weak candidates retired before human testing;
* information obtained per participant-minute;
* accuracy of parameter and model recovery;
* frequency of avoidable floor, ceiling and identifiability failures;
* correspondence between prospective computational predictions and observed human outcomes;
* cost of reaching an advance, revise or retire decision.

The predefined conventional reference workflow is specified in the Computational and AI Technical Appendix. It comprises:

manual evidence review
→ narrative candidate selection
→ fixed task design
→ conventional pilot
→ post-hoc transfer assessment

Before the efficiency evaluation begins, the team will specify whether each indicator will be assessed through a parallel comparison, a prospectively documented benchmark process or an appropriately matched historical development case.

## 3.7 Translational uncertainty addressed by the award

The principal uncertainties are whether:

* the proposed pain-related mechanisms can be measured reliably in the intended population;
* competing cognitive-affective models can be distinguished;
* computational simulations can make valid prospective predictions about human performance;
* a specific intervention configuration can modify the intended mechanism;
* mechanism change transfers to a protected, untrained task;
* any transferred change is associated with improved pain-related functioning or activity engagement;
* transferred effects remain detectable after delay;
* the intervention is acceptable, safe and sufficiently engaging;
* the product has a viable and clinically appropriate pathway position;
* the computational development engine improves intervention selection sufficiently to justify reuse and further investment.

The proposed award will bridge the gap between mechanism plausibility and a clinically credible digital product. It will determine whether the target cognitive-affective processes can be measured and modified, whether change survives protected transfer tests and delay, and whether the computational development engine improves intervention selection sufficiently to justify larger clinical and implementation studies.

# 4. Central development proposition

The proposed innovation is not simply another cognitive-training task or a fixed package of digital exercises.

The project will first define the intervention’s intended position within the chronic-primary-pain pathway:

```text
current chronic-pain pathway
→ unmet or weakly supported point
→ intended intervention user
→ access or referral route
→ clinical exclusions and escalation
→ practitioner oversight and data flow
→ patient-relevant functional outcome
→ onward care
```

Within this bounded clinical use case, the project will test whether intervention development can be improved by engineering and validating a connected adaptive policy:

```text
attend to relevant information
→ distinguish signal from noise
→ estimate uncertainty and reliability
→ revise threat or safety predictions
→ select an appropriate action
→ obtain environmental feedback
→ update the policy
→ recover it in a changed context and after delay
```

The intervention-development system will evaluate which combination of components most plausibly influences this chain and whether observed change extends beyond the trained task.

Candidate components may include:

* neutral attention-control training;
* affectively salient attention-control challenges;
* adaptive evidence accumulation;
* interpretation and appraisal updating;
* activity prediction and decision-making;
* wrapper and context variation;
* implementation intentions and real-world missions;
* protected and delayed transfer probes.

The proposed mathematics-anxiety feasibility study will provide an enabling test of the general transfer-engineering and digital-delivery methodology. Where that study proceeds and appropriate data become available, its design and results may inform learning-curve estimation, transfer-cost modelling, trial instrumentation and computational calibration. It will not determine the pain-specific intervention content or provide evidence for clinical efficacy in chronic primary pain.

# 5. Primary aims

## Aim 1: Define the target product, pathway position and mechanism model

Develop a clinically credible target product profile and pathway-placement model for adults with chronic primary pain whose functioning may be affected by maladaptive attention, appraisal, uncertainty monitoring, avoidance and inflexible updating.

Outputs:

* target patient population;
* focused map of the relevant chronic-pain pathway and unmet-support point;
* intended use, entry point and delivery pathway;
* referral or self-access route;
* candidate intervention components;
* contraindications, exclusions, escalation and safety boundaries;
* practitioner-oversight and data-flow requirements;
* implementation constraints and adoption risks;
* cognitive-affective mechanism map;
* primary mechanism, clinical and functional outcomes;
* preliminary commercialisation and implementation pathway.

## Aim 2: Build and evaluate the computational development engine

Develop and evaluate the reusable HRP Transfer Lab components required to:

* ingest relevant scientific, behavioural and intervention evidence;
* register parameter estimates with source traceability and transportability ratings;
* formalise competing cognitive-affective mechanisms;
* simulate heterogeneous, empirically constrained participant populations;
* test parameter and model recoverability;
* optimise trial conditions, task schedules and human-study designs;
* compare intervention candidates under explicit assumptions;
* generate prospective predictions for human calibration and proof-of-concept studies.

Where available, the BPS enabling study may contribute methodological calibration in accordance with Sections 1.4–1.5. It will not provide pain-specific clinical evidence.

Pain-specific priors, models and clinical interpretations will nevertheless be developed independently from chronic-pain evidence and human calibration data.

Outputs:

* auditable prior registry;
* modular generative model;
* simulation-based experiment engine;
* parameter- and model-recovery report;
* reproducible evidence packets;
* prospective human-study predictions;
* recommended candidate protocols for pain-specific testing;
* cross-domain calibration report, where enabling-study data are available;
* documented domain-adaptation framework;
* reusable registry, modelling, simulation and psychometric-development schemas.

## Aim 3: Produce and refine a pain-specific digital prototype

Translate the selected mechanism chain into a working digital intervention for research use.

A provisional intervention sequence is:

```text
brief readiness and burden check
→ controlled attention task
→ uncertainty-sensitive evidence task
→ pain-appraisal inference task
→ activity or avoidance decision
→ implementation intention
→ real-world mission
→ delayed re-check
```

Outputs:

* functioning digital prototype;
* versioned intervention protocol;
* adaptive controller;
* trial-level behavioural and timing instrumentation;
* intervention-fidelity telemetry;
* protected-transfer measures;
* patient and practitioner interfaces at prototype level;
* technical, accessibility and data-governance documentation.

## Aim 4: Conduct pain-specific product calibration and prospective candidate selection

Evaluate the reliability, acceptability, parameter recoverability and predictive validity of a small prespecified set of pain-specific measures, models and intervention candidates, and prospectively select the configuration that should enter proof-of-concept testing.

Collect dense behavioural, functional, usability and acceptability data from adults with chronic primary pain to estimate:

* task feasibility and participant burden;
* baseline parameter distributions;
* task and measure reliability;
* model and parameter recoverability;
* relationships between prespecified candidate mechanisms and pain-related functioning;
* prespecified sources of variation relevant to model calibration and candidate selection;
* predictive performance on held-out observations;
* suitability of the candidate intervention configurations for proof-of-concept testing.

This study will calibrate the computational system, compare prespecified intervention candidates and support prospective candidate selection. It will not be represented as an efficacy trial or as an open-ended investigation of chronic-pain mechanisms.

Outputs:

* pain-specific human calibration dataset;
* reliability and measurement-quality report;
* updated parameter priors;
* out-of-sample model checks;
* prospectively selected intervention mechanism and configuration;
* prospectively locked intervention configuration and comparator set;
* refined proof-of-concept study design.

## Aim 5: Conduct an early proof-of-concept comparison

Compare a small number of computationally prioritised intervention configurations with an appropriate mechanistic comparator and active control.

Primary questions:

* Can the intended cognitive-affective mechanism be modified?
* Does change extend to a protected, untrained task?
* Is the observed pattern more consistent with the intended mechanism than with competing explanations?
* Is there preliminary evidence of improvement in pain interference, activity engagement or self-management?
* Is the intervention feasible, acceptable and safe?
* Are transferred effects recoverable after delay?
* Do prospective computational predictions correspond to observed human outcomes?
* Does the evidence justify advancing, revising or retiring the intervention?

Outputs:

* proof-of-mechanism estimates;
* protected-transfer results;
* preliminary functional-outcome estimates;
* delayed-outcome results;
* feasibility, adherence and safety evidence;
* prediction-versus-observation report;
* advance, revise or retire decision package;
* specification for a later clinical evaluation.

# 8. Principal progression milestones

The project will use timed progression milestones with prespecified advance, revise and stop criteria. Progression will depend on the quality and interpretability of the evidence rather than on obtaining uniformly positive results.

## Milestone 1 — Development readiness

**Proposed timing:** completion of the clinical-specification, pathway-placement, computational-development and initial prototype phases.

### Advance criteria

* the target product profile and focused pathway-placement model have been approved by the clinical and lived-experience groups;
* the intended pathway entry point, access route, exclusions, escalation procedures and practitioner-oversight requirements have been specified;
* the core digital task sequence is functioning reliably;
* the intervention has a clinically acceptable safety and escalation protocol;
* model- and parameter-recovery performance meets prespecified parameter-specific thresholds;
* at least two prespecified competing cognitive-affective models can be distinguished under the proposed experimental design;
* the statistical design supports an informative chronic-primary-pain product-calibration study;
* relevant IP, data-access and governance issues have credible routes to resolution;
* the generic transfer-analysis pipeline has been demonstrated using the mathematics-anxiety enabling-study design, an equivalent simulated design or another appropriate calibration case.

Where the proposed BPS mathematics-anxiety study proceeds and appropriately governed data become available, it may provide a separate methodological calibration case for assessing whether the computational pipeline can produce interpretable estimates of:

* learning trajectories;
* directional-transfer costs;
* lapse and response-variability parameters;
* delayed-retention and recovery patterns;
* uncertainty around individual differences;
* the informativeness of alternative task conditions and schedules.

Progression will not depend on the BPS study being funded, completed or producing a positive transfer effect. A null or ambiguous finding may still provide useful methodological information where it identifies measurement weaknesses, rejects an implausible model, reveals uninformative conditions or supports a clear revise-or-retire decision.

The BPS study will not determine the pain-specific intervention content, clinical interpretation or progression decision. Pain-specific readiness will be established through chronic-pain evidence, clinical and lived-experience input, prototype development and the planned pain-specific calibration work.

### Revise or stop criteria

* the target constructs cannot be measured with acceptable reliability;
* the prespecified candidate models remain non-identifiable under feasible study conditions;
* the prototype produces unacceptable cognitive, emotional or practical burden;
* lived-experience contributors judge the intervention rationale or delivery format to be unacceptable;
* no credible and clinically appropriate pathway position can be identified;
* the clinical recruitment or delivery pathway is unavailable;
* the computational workflow cannot generate interpretable or prospectively testable predictions;
* the pain-specific development programme cannot proceed without relying on unsupported cross-domain assumptions from the enabling study.

## Milestone 2 — Pain-specific product calibration and candidate lock

**Proposed timing:** completion of WP4.

### Advance criteria

* recruitment and retention are feasible;
* the core pain-specific task measures meet predefined reliability and data-quality thresholds;
* device and timing effects are sufficiently controlled or modelled;
* the prespecified competing models reproduce key behavioural patterns and pass prespecified out-of-sample checks;
* at least one prespecified candidate mechanism shows sufficient measurement quality, modifiability and functional relevance to justify intervention testing;
* the intervention configuration, mechanistic comparator and active control can be selected prospectively using the prespecified decision framework;
* the selected components are distinguishable from generic engagement, expectancy and repeated-practice effects;
* the selection decision can be justified without open-ended post-hoc exploration or reliance on an unprespecified subgroup profile;
* no material safety, burden or acceptability problem has been identified.

Where available, methodological lessons from the BPS enabling study may inform:

* trial instrumentation;
* learning-curve estimation;
* transfer-cost modelling;
* adaptive scheduling;
* delayed-probe timing;
* browser and device-quality controls;
* treatment of lapse and response-variability parameters.

These lessons will concern the general transfer-analysis and digital-delivery methodology only. Pain-specific measures, parameter distributions, model interpretations, intervention candidates and progression decisions will be established independently in adults with chronic primary pain.

### Revise or stop criteria

* the models fail prespecified out-of-sample predictive checks;
* the tasks are dominated by floor, ceiling, comprehension or device-timing problems;
* none of the prespecified candidate mechanisms shows sufficient measurement quality, modifiability or functional relevance to justify intervention testing;
* the selected components cannot be distinguished from non-specific digital engagement, expectancy or repeated practice;
* no intervention configuration and comparator set can be selected without post-hoc optimisation;
* the findings depend primarily on an unprespecified participant subgroup or response profile;
* recruitment, retention, safety or acceptability are inadequate for proof-of-concept testing;
* pain-specific validity cannot be established independently of the mathematics-anxiety enabling study.


## Milestone 3 — Early proof-of-concept endpoint

### Progression criteria

* the intended mechanism changes relative to the active control or mechanistic comparator;
* change extends to a protected, untrained measure;
* the observed effect is not explained solely by trained-task practice;
* the pattern of results is more consistent with the proposed mechanism than with prespecified alternatives;
* preliminary functional outcomes are in the predicted direction;
* the intervention is feasible, acceptable and sufficiently adhered to;
* delayed assessment supports continued development;
* prospective computational predictions correspond sufficiently with observed human outcomes, or failures of prediction produce an interpretable revision to the model;
* the proposed clinical, pathway-integration, commercial and regulatory routes remain viable.

The project will conclude with one of three decisions:

* **Advance:** proceed to a larger clinical efficacy and implementation evaluation.
* **Revise:** modify the intervention components, mechanism model, measures, dosage or target population before repeating proof-of-mechanism work.
* **Retire:** discontinue the clinical intervention while retaining any validated measures, computational tools or transferable methodological assets.

# 15. Evaluation of intervention-development efficiency

The project will not assume that computational modelling, AI-assisted evidence synthesis or simulated experiments necessarily improve development efficiency.

It will prospectively evaluate the HRP Transfer Lab against the predefined conventional reference workflow described in the Computational and AI Technical Appendix. The evaluation design will specify whether individual indicators are assessed through a parallel comparison, a prospectively documented benchmark process or an appropriately matched historical development case.

```text
manual evidence review
→ narrative candidate selection
→ fixed task and protocol design
→ conventional pilot study
→ post-hoc transfer assessment
```

The comparison will assess whether the computational workflow produces measurable improvements in the informativeness, reproducibility and resource efficiency of early intervention development. Before evaluation begins, the team will specify whether each indicator will be assessed through a parallel comparison, a prospectively documented benchmark process, structured expert review or an appropriately matched historical development case.

Candidate indicators include:

* time from evidence synthesis to a prospectively locked intervention protocol;
* number of candidate protocols retired before patient testing;
* number of human studies or participants required per retained candidate;
* information obtained per participant-minute;
* accuracy and precision of parameter and model recovery;
* accuracy and calibration of prospective human predictions;
* frequency of avoidable floor, ceiling, convergence and non-identifiability problems;
* number of protocol revisions required following human calibration;
* cost and time required to reach an advance, revise or retire decision;
* number of implementation uncertainties resolved before larger clinical evaluation;
* reproducibility, source traceability and auditability of the development process.

Where the proposed BPS mathematics-anxiety study proceeds and appropriately governed outputs become available, it may provide a separate methodological calibration case for the general computational pipeline. Its design and data may be used to assess whether the pipeline can:

* reproduce a prespecified digital-intervention study design;
* recover learning trajectories and simulated directional-transfer effects;
* estimate lapse, response-variability, transfer-cost and delayed-retention parameters;
* identify uninformative or poorly identifiable task conditions;
* predict plausible patterns of human variability;
* compare alternative task schedules and trial-allocation strategies;
* generate prospective predictions that can be compared with observed study outcomes.

Progression of the chronic-primary-pain programme will not depend on the BPS study being funded, completed or producing a positive transfer effect. A null or ambiguous BPS result may still provide useful methodological evidence where it identifies measurement weaknesses, model confusion, uninformative conditions or limitations in the computational workflow.

Any analysis of the BPS study will be reported as **cross-domain methodological calibration**. It will not be treated as evidence that the HRP Transfer Lab improves the efficiency of chronic-pain intervention development, nor as evidence for the clinical validity or efficacy of the pain intervention.

The primary efficiency evaluation will therefore be conducted within the pain-specific development programme, using prospectively defined indicators and documented comparisons between predicted and observed development outcomes.

The intended claim is:

> The project will test whether computational screening and simulation-based experimental design make early pain-specific cognitive-affective intervention development more informative, reproducible and resource-efficient than a predefined conventional reference workflow.

# 19. Staged and contingency funding strategy

The MRC Proof of Concept scheme will remain the primary funding route when the chronic-primary-pain prototype, clinical partnership, computational demonstration, PPI, recruitment pathway and preliminary pain-relevant human evidence are sufficiently mature.

The proposed BPS Small Research Grant is a separate enabling award. It may de-risk the general transfer-analysis methodology, digital delivery, trial instrumentation and computational-calibration workflow, but it cannot substitute for pain-specific product development, clinical partnership, PPI or preliminary evidence in adults with chronic primary pain.

The funding strategy will therefore follow the principle:

> **Dual preparation, single submission.**

The team may initially prepare the MRC Proof of Concept route and its primary backup in parallel. A late-August route-preparation review and an early-September submission gate will determine which single principal route should be pursued.

## 19.1 Primary route: MRC Proof of Concept

The full MRC Proof of Concept application will be pursued where the readiness package demonstrates:

```text
defined pain-specific clinical product
+ pain-relevant working prototype
+ substantive clinical partnership
+ initial pain-specific PPI
+ credible recruitment and delivery pathway
+ computational and measurement demonstration
+ preliminary pain-relevant human evidence
+ prospectively defined proof-of-concept design
```

The Proof of Concept programme would support the integrated development and early evaluation of:

* the pain-specific digital intervention;
* the enabling HRP Transfer Lab methodology;
* pain-specific product calibration and candidate selection;
* an active-controlled proof-of-concept comparison;
* protected and delayed transfer outcomes;
* functional outcomes;
* pathway placement and the route to patient benefit.

A later MRC Proof of Concept round will be preferred over a premature submission where the full readiness threshold has not been met.

## 19.2 Primary backup route: MRC Impact Acceleration Award

If the full Proof of Concept readiness threshold is not met, the primary backup route will be an MRC Impact Acceleration Award focused on **one critical pain-specific development gap**.

The provisional single high-risk development step is:

> **Early clinical validation of one pain-specific cognitive-affective intervention module and its associated measurement model in adults with chronic primary pain.**

The award would determine whether the selected module:

* can be delivered reliably;
* is acceptable and sufficiently low burden;
* produces interpretable behavioural data;
* supports adequate parameter and model recovery;
* engages the prespecified target sufficiently to justify a later Proof of Concept programme.

The Impact Acceleration award would not attempt to fund:

* the complete multi-component intervention;
* the full HRP Transfer Lab architecture;
* a broad mechanism-discovery programme;
* comprehensive pathway implementation;
* a definitive proof-of-concept comparison.

Its endpoint would be a clear **advance, revise or retire** decision and the critical pain-specific evidence required for a later MRC Proof of Concept application.

The exact development step will be selected following initial prototype work and clinical consultation. Candidate options include:

* early testing of a pain-appraisal updating module;
* validation of a pain-specific measurement and computational model; or
* preliminary chronic-pain calibration of the adaptive evidence task.

Only one of these development steps will be included in the Impact Acceleration application.

```text
NIHR Programme Development Grant

= focused human mechanistic experiment

Later NIHR EME, HTA, i4i or PGfAR
= larger efficacy, implementation or technology-evaluation programme
```

## 19.5 Route-selection principle

The funding route will be selected according to the principal unresolved question.

```text
Full product and evidence package ready
→ MRC Proof of Concept

One critical pain-specific uncertainty remains
→ MRC Impact Acceleration Award

Additional pain-specific readiness work is achievable without an immediate application
→ later MRC Proof of Concept round

Focused NHS feasibility study is ready
→ NIHR Research for Patient Benefit

Preparatory programme work is required for a future PGfAR application
→ NIHR Programme Development Grant

Precise mechanistic human experiment is the primary objective
→ MRC Experimental Medicine

Service delivery or pathway redesign is the primary question
→ NIHR HSDR
```

The status or outcome of the BPS enabling study may inform the route decision by contributing methodological evidence, but it will not determine whether the chronic-primary-pain programme is ready.

All funding details, deadlines, institutional eligibility requirements and restrictions on overlapping or related applications will be rechecked with King’s pre-award and the relevant funders before the late-August route-preparation review.
